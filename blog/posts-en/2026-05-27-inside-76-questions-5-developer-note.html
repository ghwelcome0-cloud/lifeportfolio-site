How This Assessment Was Built, and Where It's Going

Closing the series with a developer's note rather than an ad. Origin, trial-and-error, limits, and the next 12 months.

May 27, 2026 · 9-min read · Series 5 of 5 (final)

1. Where it began

This tool did not begin on a whiteboard in a large conference room. It was built on one person's desk, as the accumulated result of a long stretch of time.

In college, attending a seminar based on Hyrum Smith's The 10 Natural Laws of Successful Time and Life Management led me to start using a professional planner. As my faith took shape over the following years, I went through countless trials and errors of organizing, searching for, and trying to live out my mission, vision, identity, and strengths. Life Portfolio is the tool that grew out of that accumulated time.

So this assessment did not start as a theory in academia. It is the structured residue of pages written, erased, and rewritten in a personal planner. That is both a strength and a limit. (The limits are covered in Part 4.)

2. What we drew from

The starting point was personal notes, but the structure drew on the following:

  • Hyrum Smith's value-based time management — the flow from values → goals → daily action.
  • Stephen R. Covey, The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People — mission statements and the priority matrix.
  • Amy Wrzesniewski's Job / Career / Calling distinction — three lenses on work.
  • Bryan Dik & Ryan Duffy (2009), the three-dimension theory of calling — transcendent summons, meaning & purpose, prosocial.
  • Carol Ryff's six-dimensional psychological well-being, Ed Diener's SWLS, Michael Steger's MLQ — external check-points for domain design.

These are references, not claims that we are a successor study of those scholars. We borrow shoulders, not authority.

3. The person who built it — credentials and limits

The operator, Kim Youngsik, holds a Korean Lifelong Education Specialist (평생교육사) license. Not a clinical psychology license. Not a vocational counselor license. Not a doctoral degree. That's the accurate statement.

1:1 coaching is delivered directly by the operator, but only within what a Lifelong Education Specialist license is competent to address: life design, self-understanding, and learning curation. Medical or clinical work is out of scope.

A credential tells you, more precisely than what someone can do, what they must not do. Referring out beyond the credential's boundary is an obligation the credential places on us.

5. Roadmap for the next 12 months

Next quarter (within 3 months)

  • External 5-person panel face-validity review — target: raise Tier B
  • Terms & refund policy revision, WCAG AA closure — target: raise Tier C
  • Small-run diary v1.4.2 prototype print (20–50 copies) — assess bundling with the assessment

Within 6 months

  • 100 paying respondents collected → first attempt at Cronbach's α and factor analysis (Tier D begins)
  • Report v3.0 — post-hoc weight adjustment, improved per-domain comparison visuals
  • English report in production (Korean is primary today)

Within 12 months

  • First test-retest reliability report — same respondents reassessed at 3-month intervals
  • External advisory panel (life design, lifelong education, career counseling)
  • Add Part 6 to this series (validation results) or release as a separate report

6. Closing the 5-part series

This series was written to read as a manual, not as content marketing disguised as transparency. Part 1 disclosed all 11 domains, Part 2 the four-axis mapping, Part 3 the weighting system, Part 4 the places we don't reach. Part 5 is the story on the desk where it was built.

We show all of this because — for a tool sold at ₩9,900 to organize someone's life into a single picture, the buyer's right to know what they are buying matters more than the seller's convenience. Information rights of the receiver come before the comfort of the maker.

This tool may not be right for you. We wrote these five articles so you can decide that accurately. If you conclude it's not right, that's also a good outcome.

A closing line

We live in a time when attempts to organize one's life onto a single sheet of paper grow rarer. May this assessment be a small tool that returns that sheet to your desk. What gets written on it — your hand and your time still decide.

If you've read all five parts to the end, you are the person best positioned to judge whether this tool fits your desk.
