To carry discovery all the way to ‘legacy’ — the way of one person of old

There is an old story that shows ‘from discovery to legacy’ all at once. It is the record of a man named Nehemiah.

He heard the news of broken-down walls, and his heart grew heavy (discover). He did not stop at vaguely grieving; he organized concretely what to do, when, and how, and made a plan (structure). Then, together with others, he actually rebuilt the wall and finished it in 52 days (live it out). What he left behind was not merely a wall, but a place where a whole community could stand again (leave it behind).

“I had not told anyone what my God had put in my heart to do for Jerusalem …” — Nehemiah did not let what he held in his heart drift away; he moved it into a plan and saw it through. — Nehemiah 2:12 (summarized)

The grain to learn here is clear. A discovery can be lived out when it is structured into a plan, and what is lived out flows to someone only when it becomes ‘legacy.’ This is why Life Portfolio's five steps are designed not to stop at ‘one booklet of a report’ but to carry on into living it out and leaving it behind. (Nehemiah's leadership is treated in various theological and practical works as ‘a model of recording, structuring, and executing a vision.’)

The end of ‘legacy’ — so that yet another person can live it out

So where does ‘legacy’ head? The end of legacy that Life Portfolio envisions is not ‘a record of my success.’ It is the trace of what I lived out becoming a small starting line for yet another person to live out their own place.

So in the far-off picture there is a place of loose companionship where each person's discovery and living-out become a model for one another. One person's legacy helps another's discovery, and that person's living-out flows to yet another — toward that structure, where assets spread from person to person, we move one step at a time.
