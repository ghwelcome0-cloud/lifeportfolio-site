Career questions in your 30s — what to do before another personality test

“Stay or move?” Career drift in your 30s rarely gets resolved by yet another personality test. The same type comes back. Something else needs to come first.

2026-05-15 · 6 min read · Career

What's really going on at 30+

Career drift in your 30s rarely happens because you don't know your type. Usually three lines are blurred at the same time:

  • ① Mission — what you work for (you can't say it in one line)
  • ② Strengths — what you work with (the last five years feel scattered)
  • ③ Next action — what changes this week (the calendar is empty)

Take another personality test while those three are blurred, and the same type comes back. The label sharpens; the decision does not.

What to do first — a single self-management booklet

Step 1 in your 30s is to compress “what kind of person am I going to live out this quarter?” into a single booklet. Format stays simple:

  1. One mission line — “I am someone who ______ for the sake of ______.”
  2. One vision line — “In a year, I'm known for ______.”
  3. Top 3 strengths + Top 2 growth points
  4. Three first actions this week
  5. One 3-week routine card

When those five fit in your hand, the “stay or move?” decision gets lighter — because the criterion shifts from salary to the mission line.

Why type tests stop working as you cross 30

At 22, a type test is genuinely useful — it gives you your first language about yourself. At 32, you already have 5–10 years of data: roles you loved, roles you couldn't bear, roles that were surprisingly light. What you need now is a tool that compresses that data into one line, not a tool that gives you another label.

30s career-check, 5 lines

  • ① In your current seat, what % of your mission line is being met?
  • ② Of your Top 3 strengths, how many are actually used this quarter?
  • ③ Are your Top 2 growth points being avoided or trained on purpose?
  • ④ Are the “three first actions this week” on your calendar?
  • ⑤ Is there a one-line 3-month check criterion?

If three or more answers are “no,” what you need is not a new company. It's a new booklet.

From real practice (anonymized)

The founder has run the same exercise by hand for 12 years, since 2014, just before his 30s. The same person re-issues one booklet a year, and somewhere around year five, “this seat isn't my mission” stops being a wobble. In a city church's leadership team season, in a young-adult community whose team project ended with an original track produced, performed, and released, and in BELT, a team-level diagnose-and-practice circle, the first thing each group did was receive that one-line mission sentence.

The answer to a 30s career question is rarely a new company. It's a new line.

First action — five minutes from now

Stop what you're doing for five minutes. On a blank page, write two lines:

  • ① I am someone who ______ for the sake of ______.
  • ② In a year, I'm known for ______.
